Tipp encounters the group "Resonance".. bunch of hippies that use the landscape as their instrument.. 

Tipp had always suspected that the world was held together by a series of very large, very invisible rubber bands, and that occasionally, someone accidentally plucked one.

They discovered this for certain about three miles outside the village of Oakhaven, when the air began to feel like it was being gently massaged by a giant. It wasn't a sound, exactly. It was more of a *suggestion* of a sound, the kind of noise a mountain makes when it’s thinking about moving.

Tipp stopped. Thry looked at their boots. They were vibrating. Not because Tipp was shaking, but because the ground beneath them had decided that being still was a boring way to spend a Tuesday.

"Svell-tide," muttered a local farmer, who was leaning against a fence and looking remarkably relaxed, as if he were currently being marinated in a warm bath. "The group *Resonance* is playing the Great Circle today. Proper high-gravity set. Feel that in your marrow, don't you?"

Tipp did. In fact, they felt it in their teeth.

As they entered the concert zone, they noticed the Bells. Those were small, silver things, hanging from every eaves and branch like frozen raindrops. They weren't being rung; there were no clappers, no wind. And yet, they were singing. They were humming in a microtonal harmony that made Tipp feel as though their soul were being meticulously ironed.

"It's the lithophones," the farmer explained, pointing toward the horizon where a ring of monoliths stood, looking like a set of prehistoric crooked teeth. "The big stones. They don't play music for the ears, see. They play it for the *planet*. They pump the Low-Svells, the deep-down thrums, right into the bedrock. The earth carries it. The bells just... catch the overflow. Like a bucket catching rain, only the rain is a C-sharp from three counties over."

Tipp approached the Great Circle, and there they were: *Resonance*.

They weren't so much musicians as they were raggedy mathematicians with a penchant for expensive wood. At the center stood the Svell.. an instrument that used to be called the silverwell, in the time of Tombini. The name had shortened with use..  
To the uninitiated, a svell looked like a piano that had been designed by someone who had a profound disagreement with the concept of a straight line. It was a mahogany well of impossible depth, strung with silver-silk membranes that looked less like strings and more like frozen spider-webs.

And then there were more normal instruments, that weren't custom built by the group for this sle ific location during a three weeks Camp out..
A drone pipe.. that someone would spit and sing into at the same time.. like a didgeridoo had a baby with a Waldhoorn.
And 'normal' instrument like a harp and a klappa. That were destined to be drowned out and act more like a conductor, gently pointing out the places the melody and rithm could go.

The performers didn't just play; they *negotiated* with each other, with the earth, with the instruments. Tipp watched a woman’s hand glide across the keys. Sometimes she touched them with the lightness of a moth, and the Svell produced a "Ghost" note, a sound so thin it felt like a memory of a sound. Then, without warning, she slammed a chord with a violent, focused intensity, that they felt in their toenails.

The instrument didn't just sound; it *shattered*. 

The sound was a metallic bloom, a cascading waterfall of harmonics that didn't just hit the ear, it warped the air. Tipp felt the "gravity" of the note. It was as if the sound had a physical weight, creating a hollow space in the atmosphere that the other instruments and a choir of voices that sounded like they were singing from the bottom of a very clean lake, rushed to fill.

"They're finding the gaps," the farmer whispered, sounding almost religious. "Looking for the resonance. I took music when I was a child.. You see.. if they hit the right ratio, the holy 'Simple Fraction,' they call it, the whole valley starts to vibrate in sympathy. It’s a bit like tuning a giant tuning fork only the fork is the crust of the world and you strike it with a few tons of granite."

Tipp leaned back against a smaller stone. He could feel the rhythm now, not as a beat, but as a slow, pulsing tide, a rhythmic "pitch" that faded in and out like the breathing of a sleeping god. They never learned much about music, but they knew that they were currently being shifted from one time signature to another, and that for the first time in their life, he didn't mind being a bit out of step.
