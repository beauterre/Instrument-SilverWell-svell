Tipp had always suspected that the world was held together by a series of very large, very invisible rubber bands, and that occasionally, someone accidentally plucked one.

He discovered this for certain about three miles outside the village of Oakhaven, when the air began to feel like it was being gently massaged by a giant. It wasn't a sound, exactly. It was more of a *suggestion* of a sound—the kind of noise a mountain makes when it’s thinking about moving.

Tipp stopped. He looked at his boots. They were vibrating. Not because he was shaking, but because the ground beneath them had decided that being still was a boring way to spend a Tuesday.

"Svell-tide," muttered a local farmer, who was leaning against a fence and looking remarkably relaxed, as if he were currently being marinated in a warm bath. "The group *Resonance* is playing the Great Circle today. Proper high-gravity set. Feel that in your marrow, don't you?"

Tipp did. In fact, he felt it in his teeth.

As he entered the concert zone, he noticed the Bells. They were small, silver things, hanging from every eaves and branch like frozen raindrops. They weren't being rung; there were no clappers, no wind. And yet, they were singing. They were humming in a microtonal harmony that made Tipp feel as though his soul were being meticulously ironed.

"It's the lithophones," the farmer explained, pointing toward the horizon where a ring of monoliths stood, looking like a set of prehistoric teeth. "The big stones. They don't play music for the ears, see. They play it for the *planet*. They pump the Low-Swells—the deep-down thrums—right into the bedrock. The earth carries it. The bells just... catch the overflow. Like a bucket catching rain, only the rain is a C-sharp from three counties over."

Tipp approached the Great Circle, and there they were: *Resonance*.

They weren't so much musicians as they were mathematicians with a penchant for expensive wood. At the center stood the Svells. To the uninitiated, they looked like pianos that had been designed by someone who had a profound disagreement with the concept of a straight line. They were mahogany wells of impossible depth, strung with silver-silk membranes that looked less like strings and more like frozen spider-webs.

The performers didn't just play; they *negotiated* with the instruments. Tipp watched a woman’s hand glide across the keys. Sometimes she touched them with the lightness of a moth, and the Svell produced a "Ghost" note—a sound so thin it felt like a memory of a sound. Then, without warning, she slammed a chord with a violent, focused intensity.

The instrument didn't just sound; it *shattered*. 

The sound was a metallic bloom, a cascading waterfall of harmonics that didn't just hit the ear—it warped the air. Tipp felt the "gravity" of the note. It was as if the sound had a physical weight, creating a hollow space in the atmosphere that the other instruments—a deep, subterranean didgeridoo and a choir of voices that sounded like they were singing from the bottom of a very clean lake—rushed to fill.

"They're finding the gaps," the farmer whispered, sounding almost religious. "Looking for the resonance. If they hit the right ratio—the 'Simple Fraction,' they call it—the whole valley starts to vibrate in sympathy. It’s a bit like tuning a giant guitar, only the guitar is the crust of the world and the pick is a few tons of granite."

Tipp leaned back against a smaller stone. He could feel the rhythm now—not as a beat, but as a slow, pulsing tide, a rhythmic "pitch" that faded in and out like the breathing of a sleeping god. He didn't know much about music, but he knew that he was currently being shifted from one time signature to another, and that for the first time in his life, he didn't mind being a bit out of step.
